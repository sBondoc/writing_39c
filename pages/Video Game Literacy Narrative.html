    I have been exposed to video games for most of my conscious life. Some of my earliest memories involve my little brother and I sitting around the computer watching our dad and older brother play StarCraft and Warcraft III on the computer, mesmerized by the lights and sounds coming from what we could only describe as a smaller TV. We did not understand what allured us to it so much; we could not even read the words on the screen or understand half of the words coming from the game's audio. Perhaps it was the bonding aspect of all the boys in our family congregating over a common spectacle, one that entertained us on the TV screen in the lobby for the hours-long layover we had in Korea on our way to the Philippines when we were young. It was during that very trip that my older brother bought Pokmon Diamond, the first video game I would ever find myself playing. Something about exploring the world and leveling up my Onix captivated my attention like nothing else had, and with nothing much to do at home after what little homework a first-grader could have, I spent hours on that DS, catching Pokmon, learning about the game mechanics, and finding a sort of freedom in those two small screens with buttons that I just was not able to get in the real world before. After a few years, I moved on to the "big boy" games, the ones my older brother and dad had been playing. I picked up StarCraft when I was around 7, and I was absolutely horrible about it. But I was being like my dad, like my older brother, and I loved it. I moved to creating games of my own through the game's level editor, which is what I began to spend most of my time on. Once StarCraft II rolled out, the tools at my disposal in the new level editor let me express my creativity even further, developing the hobby of mapmaking and coding that I pursue today. As I grew older, I became better at the base game itself and found myself enjoying its competitive aspect, making my own foray into ladder matches and learning strategies by watching professional matches religiously. Nowadays, I do not have as much time to play as I used to, but on occasion I do play matches here and there and replay the campaign. When I go home I sometimes watch Starcraft II tournaments with my dad and my brothers when they are around, much as other families might watch football or other sports. Video games will always have a nostalgic aspect to them, and the ability for them to stimulate my creativity, entertain, and engage makes them something worth looking forward to.
